An Introduction to Bayesian Data Analysis for Cognitive Science

Chapter 4 Introduction to Bayesian data analysis computationally

In the previous chapter, we learned how to derive the posterior distribution of our model analytically. In practice, however, this is possible for a very limited number of cases. While the numerator of the Bayes rule, the unnormalized posterior, is easy to calculate (either by multiplying analytically the probability density/mass functions or even by multiplying the dbinom, or other d* functions in R), the denominator, the marginal likelihood, requires us to integrate the numerator; see (4.1).

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{y}) &= \cfrac{ p(\boldsymbol{y}|\boldsymbol{\theta}) \cdot p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) }{p(\boldsymbol{y})}\\ p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{y}) &= \cfrac{ p(\boldsymbol{y}|\boldsymbol{\theta}) \cdot p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) }{\int_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} p(\boldsymbol{y}|\boldsymbol{\theta}) \cdot p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) d\boldsymbol{\theta} } \end{aligned} \tag{4.1} \end{equation}\]

Unless we are dealing with conjugate distributions, the solution will be extremely hard to derive or there will be none. This was the major bottleneck of Bayesian analysis in the past, and required Bayesian practitioners to program an approximation method by themselves before they could even begin the Bayesian analysis. Fortunately, many of the probabilistic programming languages freely available today (see the next section for a listing) allow us define our models without having to acquire expert knowledge about the relevant numerical techniques.
